Although the AlphaFold code and trained models are publicly available through the GitHub repository, fully replicating the original study’s findings is challenging for several reasons. First, the model was trained using extremely large and complex datasets, including the Protein Data Bank (PDB), UniRef90, BFD, Uniclust30, and MGnify, which together contain billions of protein sequences; recreating the exact versions and preprocessing of these datasets is difficult and can significantly affect model performance. Additionally, the training process required substantial computational resources—specifically, 128 TPU v3 cores and millions of training samples over approximately one week plus further fine-tuning—which are not readily accessible to most researchers. The methodology also involved complex preprocessing pipelines, including the generation of multiple sequence alignments using specialized tools and the construction of large custom databases such as the Big Fantastic Database (BFD), making exact replication technically demanding. Furthermore, the final model incorporated a self-distillation strategy that used predicted structures from hundreds of thousands of additional sequences, a procedure that is difficult to reproduce precisely. Variability introduced by stochastic training with different random seeds, as well as dependence on specific versions of sequence and structural databases, can also lead to differences in results even when using the same code. Consequently, while researchers can reproduce structure predictions using the released models, replicating the exact training process and performance reported in the study remains impractical for most environments.
